I am a doctoral candidate in Literary and Cultural Studies at Carnegie Mellon University. My research focuses on questions of transnational identities and the meanings of home and belonging. Specifically, I examine how diasporic experiences and transnational sensibilities shape identities that are either deterritorialized or possess the potential for deterritorialization. I use diasporic narratives as my primary sources, and through engagement with critical race studies, postcolonial and transnational studies, mobility studies, Black geographies, and digital humanities methodologies, I explore how literature and cultural representations reveal alternative ways of inhabiting and reimagining home spaces, particularly within urban environments.  

My dissertation, titled "Urban Elsewhere: Diasporic Imagination and Narratives of Transnationalism," investigates representations of diasporan individuals navigating territorialized and contested urban spaces. These individuals often rewrite hidden or overlooked histories of cities, transforming liminal spaces—such as highways and borders—into what I term “urban elsewhere”: porous, fluid counter-spaces that challenge dominant narratives of spatial belonging. This four-chapter project includes close readings of Karen Tei Yamashita’s *Tropic of Orange *(1997), Teju Cole’s *Open City* (2011), Dionne Brand’s *Love Enough* (2014), and Elamin Abdelmahmoud’s *Son of Elsewhere: A Memoir in Pieces* (2022), uncovering how urban spaces become sites of racial governance and resistance.

My research and teaching interests are **Diaspora Literature, Hemispheric Studies, Multiethnic American Literature Post-1945, Asian American Literature, Digital Humanities, Urban Fiction, Life-Writing, Mobility Studies, Transnational Studies, Biopolitics**.

Recent Project
======
[Diaspora Voices in Translation: Building a Digital Archive of Translated Works Post-9/11](https://next-test-one-henna.vercel.app/) is an ongoing project tracing and charting the transnational (US-Korea) migratory journey of diasporic narratives. In this project, I focus specifically on novels written by diasporan authors based in the United States, whose works are published in English and later translated into Korean and released in the Korean book market. The project centers on novels published post-9/11, and the archive will be updated annually. The archive includes bibliographic data and blurbs for each work. For more information on this project, or if you wish to collaborate, please don't hesitate to reach out to me!
