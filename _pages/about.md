I'm a doctoral candidate in Literary and Cultural Studies at Carnegie Mellon University. My dissertation explores diasporic experiences and counter-spaces, which I name "urban elsehwere," within major North American urban centers. It challenges dominant narratives that shape how urban spaces are imagined through the language of and for territorialization, perpetuating the past and present practices of exclusion and inclucion, and, instead, proposes alternative modes of living together through close readings of contemporary novels and a memoir authored by individuals of African, Caribbean, or Asian descent -- works by Karen Tei Yamashita, Teju Cole, Dionne Brand, and Elamin Abdelmahmoud. In the closing chapter of my dissertation, I bring together the previous discussions on diasporic experiences and sensibilities to expand on the concept of "urban elsewhere" in terms of readership and book consumption.   

My research and teaching interests are **Diaspora Literature, Hemispheric Studies, Multiethnic American Literature Post-1945, Digital Humanities, Urban Fiction, Life-Writing, Transnational Studies, Biopolitics**.

[Diaspora Voices in Translation: Building a Digital Archive of Translated Works Post-9/11](https://next-test-one-henna.vercel.app/)
======
This is an ongoing project tracing and charting the transnational (US-Korea) migratory journey of diasporic narratives. In this project, I focus specifically on novels written by diasporan authors based in the United States, whose works are published in English and later translated into Korean and released in the Korean book market. The project centers on novels published post-9/11, and the archive will be updated annually. The archive includes bibliographic data and blurbs for each work. For more information on this project, or if you wish to collaborate, please don't hesitate to reach out to me!
